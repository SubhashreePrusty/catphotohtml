Alan Mathison Turing

The man who indirectly ended the Nazi rule and shortened the Second World War

Here's a time line of Turing's life:

  • 1912 - Born in Maida Vale, London.
  • 1928 - Aged 16, encountered Albert Einstein's work and managed to deduce Einstein's questioning of Newton's laws of motion from a text.
  • 1934 - Awarded first-class honours in Mathematics after studying as an undergraduate at King's College, Cambridge.
  • 1936 - Published his paper "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem" which has been called "easily the most influential math paper in history".
  • 1938 - Obtained his PhD from the Department of Mathematics at Princeton where he also studied cryptology. Then he worked part-time with the Government Code and Cypher School (GC&CS), the British codebreaking organisation.
  • 1939 - was a leading participant in the breaking of German ciphers at Bletchley Park , during the Second World War. He concentrated on cryptanalysis of the Enigma cipher machine used by Nazi Germany, together with a senior GC&CS codebreaker.
  • 1940 - Led the section that was responsible for German naval cryptanalysis and devised a number of techniques for speeding the breaking of German ciphers, including improvements to the pre-war Polish "bombe" method, an electromechanical machine that could find settings for the Enigma machine.
  • 1941 - successfully cracked intercepted coded messages that enabled the Allies to defeat the Axis powers in many crucial engagements, including the Battle of the Atlantic.It has been estimated that this work shortened the war in Europe by more than two years and saved over 14 million lives.
  • 1946 - After the war, Turing worked at the National Physical Laboratory, where he designed the Automatic Computing Engine (ACE), one of the first designs for a stored-program computer.
  • 1952 - was prosecuted for homosexual acts. He accepted hormone treatment with DES, as an alternative to prison.
  • 1954 - dies at the age of 42. An inquest determined his death as a suicide, but it has been noted that the known evidence is also consistent with accidental poisoning.

After his death, The British Prime Minister Gordon Brown made an official public apology on behalf of the British government for "the appalling way he was treated".Queen Elizabeth II granted a posthumous pardon.
